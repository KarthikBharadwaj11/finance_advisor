INVESTMENT BASICS FOR INDIVIDUAL INVESTORS

## Asset Classes

Stocks (Equities)
Stocks represent ownership in a company. They offer higher long-term returns (historically 7–10% annually after inflation for broad US indices) but come with higher short-term volatility. Key concepts:
- Market capitalization: Large-cap stocks (>$10B) are more stable; small-cap (<$2B) have higher growth potential but more risk.
- Diversification: Owning a broad index fund (S&P 500, Total Market) eliminates single-stock risk.
- Dividend yield: Some stocks return cash quarterly. Dividend-paying stocks (utilities, REITs) tend to be less volatile.

Bonds (Fixed Income)
Bonds are loans to governments or corporations in exchange for periodic interest payments. They provide:
- Predictable income stream
- Capital preservation (lower volatility than stocks)
- Portfolio ballast during equity market downturns
- Historical returns: 2–5% annually depending on duration and credit quality
Types: Treasury bonds (safest), investment-grade corporate bonds, high-yield (junk) bonds (higher risk/return), municipal bonds (tax advantages for high earners).

Real Estate
Real estate exposure can be achieved via:
- REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts): Publicly traded, liquid. Required to distribute 90% of taxable income.
- Direct property: Higher returns possible but illiquid and management-intensive.
- Real estate historically returns 4–8% annually with inflation protection.

Cash and Cash Equivalents
Money market funds, high-yield savings accounts, CDs, Treasury bills. Preserve capital with minimal return (currently 4–5% in high-rate environments). Critical for emergency funds.

Commodities and Alternatives
Gold, oil, agricultural products. Low correlation with stocks/bonds makes them useful for diversification. However, commodities don't produce income and returns depend solely on price appreciation.

## Compound Interest: The Foundation of Wealth Building

The Rule of 72: Divide 72 by the annual return rate to estimate how many years it takes to double an investment.
- At 6% return: ~12 years to double
- At 9% return: ~8 years to double
- At 4% return: ~18 years to double

Starting early is the single most impactful decision. $10,000 invested at age 25 at 7% becomes $149,745 by age 65. The same $10,000 invested at 35 becomes only $76,123.

## Tax-Advantaged Accounts (US)

401(k): Pre-tax contributions reduce current taxable income. Employer match is free money — always contribute at least enough to capture the full match. 2024 limit: $23,000 ($30,500 if age 50+).

Roth IRA: Contributions are post-tax but withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. Best when you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement. 2024 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+). Phase-out begins at $146K (single) / $230K (married).

HSA (Health Savings Account): Triple tax advantage — deductible contributions, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for medical expenses. After age 65, withdrawals for any purpose are taxed as ordinary income (like a 401k).

## Index Funds vs Active Management

Academic research (Fama-French, SPIVA reports) consistently shows that 80–90% of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index over 15+ year periods after fees.

Index fund advantages:
- Lower fees (expense ratio 0.03–0.20% vs 0.75–1.5% for active)
- Broad diversification
- Tax efficiency (low turnover)
- Outperform most active managers over long periods

Recommended core holdings: VTI (Total US Market), VXUS (International), BND (US Bonds), or a single Target Date fund for simplicity.

## Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals regardless of market price. Benefits:
- Removes the temptation to time the market
- Automatically buys more shares when prices are low
- Reduces impact of volatility
- Builds investment discipline

Studies show DCA vs lump-sum investing: Lump-sum outperforms ~67% of the time in historical backtests, but DCA reduces regret risk and is behaviorally optimal for most investors.
