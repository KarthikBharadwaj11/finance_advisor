RETIREMENT PLANNING: A COMPLETE FRAMEWORK

## The 4% Safe Withdrawal Rate

The "4% Rule" (Bengen, 1994; Trinity Study) states that a retiree can withdraw 4% of their initial portfolio value annually, adjusted for inflation, with a high probability (>95%) of not running out of money over 30 years.

Retirement number calculation: Annual expenses × 25
- $40,000/year expenses → $1,000,000 needed
- $60,000/year expenses → $1,500,000 needed
- $80,000/year expenses → $2,000,000 needed

Updated research (Pfau, Kitces): In current low-yield environments, 3–3.5% may be more conservative and appropriate for very early retirees (60-year horizons).

## Retirement Account Types and Strategies

Traditional 401(k) / IRA:
- Pre-tax contributions reduce current taxable income
- Tax-deferred growth
- All withdrawals taxed as ordinary income in retirement
- Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) start at age 73
- Best when current tax rate > expected retirement tax rate

Roth 401(k) / Roth IRA:
- Post-tax contributions (no current tax benefit)
- Tax-free growth AND withdrawals
- No RMDs for Roth IRAs
- Best when current tax rate < expected retirement tax rate, or for young earners expecting income growth
- Also valuable for tax diversification

Tax diversification strategy: Maintain both traditional and Roth accounts to give flexibility in retirement to control taxable income (e.g., withdraw more Roth in high-tax years, more traditional in low-tax years).

## Employer Match: Non-Negotiable Free Money

A 100% employer match up to 6% of salary = an instant 100% return on those dollars. This is the highest guaranteed return available to any investor. Never leave it on the table.

Example: Salary = $80,000. Match = 100% up to 6% = $4,800/year free from employer.
Not contributing to get the match = forfeiting $4,800 in compensation.

## Retirement Savings Benchmarks by Age (Fidelity Guidelines)

- Age 30: 1× annual salary saved
- Age 35: 2× annual salary
- Age 40: 3× annual salary
- Age 45: 4× annual salary
- Age 50: 6× annual salary
- Age 55: 7× annual salary
- Age 60: 8× annual salary
- Age 67: 10× annual salary

These assume Social Security will cover ~40% of pre-retirement income (for average earners).

## Social Security Optimization

Claiming age dramatically affects lifetime benefits:
- Age 62 (earliest): Benefit reduced by up to 30% permanently
- Full Retirement Age (FRA): Age 67 for those born after 1960
- Age 70 (maximum): Benefit increased by 8%/year delay after FRA (24–32% bonus)

Break-even analysis: Delaying from 62 to 70 breaks even at approximately age 80. If you expect to live past 80, delaying is mathematically superior.

Married couples strategy: Higher-earning spouse delays to 70 to maximize the survivor benefit. Lower earner can claim earlier.

## Target Date Funds

Target date funds (e.g., Vanguard Target Retirement 2050) automatically:
- Start aggressive (90%+ stocks when far from target date)
- "Glide path" gradually shifts to more conservative as target date approaches
- End at approximately 50% stocks / 50% bonds at target date

Advantages: Set-it-and-forget-it simplicity, automatic rebalancing, low cost (~0.10–0.15% expense ratio).

Suitable for anyone who doesn't want to manage their own asset allocation.

## Healthcare in Retirement: The Hidden Risk

Medical costs are the largest and most unpredictable retirement expense:
- Average couple needs ~$315,000 for healthcare costs in retirement (Fidelity 2023 estimate)
- Medicare eligibility begins at age 65 — gap between retirement and 65 is expensive
- Long-term care (nursing home, home health aide) can cost $50,000–$100,000/year and is NOT covered by Medicare
- Long-term care insurance: Consider purchasing at age 55–65 before premiums become prohibitive

## Sequence of Returns Risk Mitigation

The first 5–10 years of retirement are the most dangerous for a portfolio. A severe market decline early in retirement can permanently impair the portfolio even if markets eventually recover.

Mitigations:
1. Cash/bond buffer: Hold 1–3 years of expenses in cash/short-term bonds; spend from this during downturns rather than selling equities
2. Flexible spending: Reduce discretionary spending by 10–15% during market downturns
3. Part-time work in early retirement: Even $20,000/year from part-time work dramatically reduces portfolio withdrawal rate
4. Annuity for base income: Consider a simple SPIA (single-premium immediate annuity) to cover essential expenses, freeing the equity portfolio for discretionary spending

## Monte Carlo Simulation Interpretation

A 90% success rate in Monte Carlo retirement planning means: In 90 out of 100 simulated scenarios using historical return distributions, the portfolio did not run out of money before the target date. A 90%+ success rate is generally considered sufficient for planning purposes.
