RISK MANAGEMENT AND RISK PROFILES IN PERSONAL FINANCE

## Defining Risk Tolerance

Risk tolerance has two components that must both be assessed:

1. Risk Capacity: Your objective ability to absorb losses based on:
   - Time horizon (longer = more capacity)
   - Income stability (stable job = more capacity)
   - Existing wealth relative to goals
   - Liquidity needs (emergency fund coverage)

2. Risk Willingness: Your psychological comfort with volatility and potential losses
   - How would you react to a 30% portfolio decline in one year?
   - Have you experienced significant investment losses before?
   - Do you check your portfolio daily or quarterly?

Risk tolerance mismatch is dangerous: An aggressive portfolio on a conservative personality leads to panic selling at market bottoms, permanently destroying wealth.

## Risk Profiles

CONSERVATIVE INVESTOR
- Age: Typically 55+ or those with short horizons
- Goal: Capital preservation with modest growth
- Typical allocation: 30% stocks / 50% bonds / 20% cash
- Max comfortable drawdown: 10–15%
- Expected return: 3–5% annually
- Suitable for: Emergency funds, money needed within 3 years, retirees

MODERATE INVESTOR
- Age: Typically 35–55, balanced growth/stability priorities
- Goal: Growth with manageable volatility
- Typical allocation: 60% stocks / 35% bonds / 5% cash
- Max comfortable drawdown: 20–30%
- Expected return: 5–7% annually
- Suitable for: Retirement 10–20 years away, medium-term goals

AGGRESSIVE INVESTOR
- Age: Typically under 40 with long horizon
- Goal: Maximum long-term growth, accepts short-term volatility
- Typical allocation: 80–90% stocks / 10–15% bonds / 5% cash/alternatives
- Max comfortable drawdown: 40–50%
- Expected return: 7–10% annually
- Suitable for: Retirement 20+ years away, wealth accumulation phase

## Key Risk Metrics

Volatility (Standard Deviation): Measures how much returns deviate from the average. Higher volatility = higher risk. S&P 500 historical annual volatility: ~15%.

Beta: Measures an investment's sensitivity to market movements. Beta of 1.0 = moves with market. Beta of 1.5 = 50% more volatile than market.

Sharpe Ratio: (Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation. Measures return per unit of risk. Higher is better. Anything above 1.0 is considered good.

Maximum Drawdown: The largest peak-to-trough decline over a period. S&P 500 max drawdown in 2008-09 was -57%. Knowing your historical max drawdown prepares investors psychologically.

Correlation: How two assets move together. Assets with low/negative correlation (like stocks and bonds) provide diversification benefits. During extreme market stress, correlations often converge to 1.0.

## Emergency Fund: The Foundation of All Financial Planning

Before investing, maintain an emergency fund of:
- 3 months expenses: Minimum for stable dual-income households
- 6 months expenses: Recommended for most people
- 9–12 months expenses: Self-employed, single income, or volatile employment

Emergency fund rules:
- Must be immediately liquid (savings account, money market)
- Never invested in stocks or anything that can decline
- Separate account to avoid spending temptation
- Replenish immediately after any withdrawal

Failure to maintain an emergency fund leads to forced investment liquidation at the worst possible times (market bottoms) or high-interest debt accumulation.

## Sequence of Returns Risk

Particularly relevant near retirement: the order in which investment returns occur matters enormously.
- Two investors with the same average return can have drastically different outcomes depending on whether poor returns occur early or late in retirement.
- Mitigation: Hold 1–3 years of expenses in cash/bonds as a "buffer" near and in retirement.
- Strategy: Bucket approach — Bucket 1 (0–3 years, cash), Bucket 2 (3–10 years, bonds), Bucket 3 (10+ years, stocks).

## Debt Risk Assessment

High-interest debt (above 6–7%) should always be prioritized before investing:
- Credit card debt at 20%+ APR: Guaranteed 20% return by paying it off
- Personal loans at 10%+: Pay off before investing
- Student loans at 5–7%: Context-dependent; may invest alongside repayment
- Mortgage at 3–4%: Typically favorable to invest instead of prepaying
- Mortgage at 7%+: Consider aggressive prepayment

Debt-to-Income ratio (DTI) thresholds:
- Below 20%: Healthy, comfortable for investing
- 20–35%: Manageable but limit new debt
- 35–50%: Concerning, focus on debt reduction
- Above 50%: Financial stress territory, debt payoff is top priority
